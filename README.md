# :loudspeaker: From 9 Pastas to 70: What Digital Marketing Really Does for Small Businesses in New Zealand

## Project Overview :exclamation::exclamation:
There is a small Italian restaurant tucked away in the hills of Aldgate, Adelaide. Great food. A passionate owner. And on a quiet Wednesday night, only nine pastas going out the door.

I came across their story watching a YouTube video by Davie Fogarty, an entrepreneur who stepped in to help turn the business around. Two weeks after posting one well-crafted Instagram video, Spaghetti Head went from nine pastas a night to over seventy. I watched the whole thing and kept thinking: I have read about exactly this.

That is when it clicked. A literature review I had completed on digital marketing and SME performance was not just an academic exercise anymore. It was a playbook. And Spaghetti Head had just run it.

This repository brings those two things together. The literature, drawn from peer-reviewed studies, and the case, a real small business that applied the principles without knowing the theory behind them. The analysis is framed through the New Zealand business environment, where SMEs carry their own set of conditions, constraints, and opportunities.

## :question: Assessment Objectives  
### 1. Connect the dots between research and reality
Academic literature on digital marketing is full of frameworks, models, and findings. The real question is whether any of it actually shows up in the way small businesses behave and grow. This project tests that by mapping the findings of the literature review against a real SME case, Spaghetti Head, to see how closely theory and practice align in action.

### 2. Build understanding of the New Zealand business environment
Coming in as an international student, adapting to a new market is one of the most important parts of becoming a credible analytics professional here. Reading global research is a starting point. Understanding how those insights apply, or need to be adjusted, for the NZ context is the real work. This project uses the literature and the case study as a way to deepen that local understanding, particularly around how NZ SMEs approach digital tools, customer relationships, and the barriers they face

## :floppy_disk: Methodology
### 1. Literature Review
The starting point was an academic literature review completed as part of a Strategic Marketing course. The review examined peer-reviewed research on digital marketing and its impact on SME performance, covering concepts like digital value creation, customer engagement, and omnichannel marketing.

### 2. Case Study Selection
While revisiting the literature, I came across a YouTube video by entrepreneur Davie Fogarty documenting his attempt to turn around Spaghetti Head, a small Italian restaurant in Aldgate, Adelaide. The case aligned closely with the themes in the literature, making it a real-world reference point for testing whether the research holds up in practice.

### 3. Connecting Theory to Practice
Each key finding from the literature was mapped against the decisions and outcomes observed in the Spaghetti Head case. The goal was to identify where theory and practice aligned, where gaps existed, and what that means for SMEs operating in the New Zealand context.

### 4. Local Context Application
Throughout the analysis, findings were filtered through the lens of the New Zealand business environment, drawing on NZ-specific studies to highlight where global research needs to be adapted for local conditions.

## :collision: Key Insights 
### 1. Visibility is the first problem to solve
Spaghetti Head had quality food and a committed owner. What held the business back was simply that people did not know it existed. The literature confirms this pattern across SMEs: digital marketing expands social presence and reach, and without it, even strong products stay undiscovered. Ash's turnaround began the moment he gave people something to find.

### 2. Content works when it carries a real story
The video that changed everything for Spaghetti Head was personal, specific, and emotionally honest. Davie framed it around a small business at risk, a founder experimenting in his kitchen, and a product worth travelling for. The literature identifies this as the "engine of customer engagemen"t: constant communication that builds trust and loyalty.

### 3. Digital value creation starts with observation
The colored pasta idea came from watching what was already resonating on Instagram. That is digital value creation in practice, using consumer signals from digital platforms to shape product and content decisions. Ash adapted a trend to fit what he could actually deliver, which is exactly what resource-constrained SMEs need to do.

### 4. The right channel matters more than the number of channels
One well-placed Instagram video outperformed years of inactivity. The literature reinforces this: digital marketing is context-dependent, and channel selection must match the business and its audience. In the New Zealand context specifically, email marketing often outperforms social media for SMEs because Kiwi business owners are cautious about the reputational risks of open public platforms.

### 5. Capability determines whether strategy becomes results
Two SMEs can use the same platform and get completely different outcomes. The Resource-Based View explains why: digital marketing only works when the people behind it have the knowledge, confidence, and organisational culture to execute consistently. For Ash, that capability came from outside through Davie's guidance. For NZ SMEs, the literature points to digital marketing training as the most practical path to building it from within.

## :sparkles: Reflection
Coming from a different country, the instinct is to treat knowledge as transferable. Learn the frameworks, apply the frameworks. That approach works up to a point, and this project showed me where that point is.

The New Zealand business environment has its own rhythm. Kiwi SMEs are cautious, community-oriented, and reputation-sensitive. Those qualities shape how digital marketing lands here in ways that a global literature review fully prepares you for only when paired with local research. Email marketing outperforms social media in many NZ sectors because Kiwi business owners are deliberate about protecting the trust they have built with their customers. That is a nuance that becomes visible only when you study the local context specifically.

Adaptation is a professional skill, and this project gave me a concrete opportunity to practise it. Understanding a new market takes more than reading about it. It takes the willingness to treat your existing knowledge as a starting point. That is the mindset I am building, one project at a time.
